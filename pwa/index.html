How to use this tool

Step 1: Getting Started

Gather your payslips & holiday forms

What to collect
  • From your email, download all your payslip PDFs for the period you want to review — ideally, your full employment.
  • Holidays:
    • Grab your holiday request/approval forms to check whether the holiday pay you received matches what you were owed.
    • Check your contract, or ask your employer to confirm your holiday allocation. Some companies offer additional days over and above the statutory minimums.
    • You will also need to know what month your holidays roll over.
Note: Ask your manager if you need a password to open your payslips, or if you are missing any dates.
How much data do I need?

The tool works with whatever payslips you have, but accuracy improves significantly with more data:

  • 3 months minimum — holiday pay checks will start to run, but the rolling average will be based on limited data. The report will flag this as limited data.
  • 12+ months — the tool can build a full 52-week reference average, giving the most accurate holiday pay checks. More is always better.
  • Pension checks — useful from any number of months, as long as you also upload the matching NEST contribution records.

If you are in your first year of employment, the tool will use however many months you have — consistent with ACAS guidance. Results are marked as limited data until 52 weeks of pay have accumulated.

Pensions: Know Your Rights

  • Eligibility
    • Auto‑enrolment: Workers must be automatically enrolled if they are aged 22 up to State Pension Age and earn £10,000 or more per year.
    • Right to opt in (with employer contributions): Workers earning between £6,240 and £9,999 per year should not be auto‑enrolled, but may opt in. Employers must contribute if they do.
    • Right to join (no employer contribution required): Workers earning below £6,240 per year may still join the pension scheme, but employers are not required to contribute.
  • Enrolment timing

    Employers must enrol eligible workers within 6 weeks (or notify if using up to 3‑month postponement).

  • Minimum contributions

    For employees with qualifying earnings between £6,240 and £50,270, the minimum pension contribution is 8% of qualifying earnings (3% from your employer and 5% from your pay).

    If you are in the NEST pension scheme, only 4% is deducted from your wage. The remaining 1% of your 5% worker contribution is added as government tax relief inside your NEST account.

    Qualifying Earnings Explained:
    Qualifying earnings are the portion of your gross pay between £6,240 and £50,270. Minimum pension contributions are calculated only on this band before any other deductions such as student loan payments, PAYE income tax, or National Insurance — not on your full wage.

  • On‑time payments

    Contributions must be paid to the pension provider by the 22nd of the following month.

  • Opt‑outs

    Must be handled through the pension provider; employer cannot pressure or pre‑fill forms.

  • Re‑enrolment

    Every 3 years employers must re‑assess and re‑enrol eligible staff.

Email your employer

If you spot discrepancies in your pay, tax, or pension contributions, the usual first step is to raise the issue with your manager and/or HR.

Email tips
  • You can start informally, but always follow up in writing. Email creates a clear timeline.
  • Keep your tone polite, curious, and factual.
  • If you have multiple issues, separate them into different emails.
    • For example:
      • One email about holiday pay
      • A separate email about pension contributions
  • This matters because holiday pay and pensions fall under different laws and regulators. Keeping them separate prevents employers from giving one blanket explanation that doesn’t address each issue properly.

Step 4: Get Support

Citizens Advice

Citizens Advice is a network of independent charities offering free, confidential, and impartial support. They can help you understand your rights at work, challenge unfair treatment, and navigate issues like unpaid wages, discrimination, redundancy, or dismissal. They also look at the bigger picture — how work problems affect your finances, benefits, or housing.

  • Scotland: Find a local branch
  • England & Wales: Find a local branch
Northern Ireland: Citizens Advice does not operate in Northern Ireland; however, they do maintain a list of like-minded resources for the region, which you can find here:
  • Support in Northern Ireland

Free workplace help and advice

If you have a problem at work or a question about your rights, you can get free, confidential, and impartial help.

If you work in England, Scotland, or Wales, you should contact Advisory, Conciliation and Arbitration Service (ACAS) or if you work in Northern Ireland, you should contact the Labour Relations Agency (LRA).

These organisations are there to help both workers and employers solve disputes over things like pay, holidays, or unfair treatment without needing to go to court. They don’t take sides and their advice is expert and up-to-date.

  • Contact ACAS
  • Contact LRA

Missing pension payments?

If after 8 weeks of notifying your employer in writing, they have still not resolved pension issues (such as missing contributions or failing to enrol eligible staff), then there are a few steps you can take:

  1. Submit a complaint:
    The Pensions Ombudsman
  2. Report your employer:
    The Pensions Regulator .
Whistleblowing

Reporting your employer to a regulator is known as whistleblowing. UK law provides protections for workers who report serious wrongdoing.

Failing to pay pension contributions or to enrol eligible employees counts as a serious breach.

You can report anonymously, but the more detail you provide, the easier it is for regulators to take action.

Join a union

If you’re not already a union member, now is a good time to join.

If your workplace already recognises a union, or colleagues are members, join the same one — collective strength matters.

In the UK, Unite has a strong presence in hospitality (among many other sectors). If you’re unsure which union fits your job, the Trades Union Congress (TUC) can help you find the right one.

Note: Many unions can’t offer full support for issues that happened before you joined, so it’s best to sign up early.
