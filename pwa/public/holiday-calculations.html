Holiday Pay Explained

Understand your holiday pay entitlement, or use the calculator below to estimate what you should have been paid.

How holidays are calculated

Hourly & Irregular Hours Workers

What you're entitled to

From April 2020: If your hours change from week to week or you only work part of the year, your holiday pay must be based on your average pay from the last 52 weeks you were paid. It cannot be a flat rate. Everyone gets at least 5.6 weeks of paid holiday a year (28 days if you work 5 days a week; less if you work fewer days).

From April 2024: The way your holiday amount is worked out changed, but the total you get stayed the same. Your entitlement is based on your average weekly hours × 5.6 weeks.

If your leave year starts on or after 1 April 2024, the report shows this as the 12.07% method, which means your holiday builds up each pay period. If your leave year started before April 2024, it shows the 5.6 weeks method. Both ways give the same total holiday over the year.

If you work fixed hours at a fixed rate, your holiday pay is simply your normal weekly pay. No averaging is needed.

How the report reads your holiday hours

Hourly payslips usually show a clear line for holiday hours and the rate you were paid for them. The report reads this line directly, so it doesn’t need to guess from the money amount.

The report also checks whether the holiday rate on your payslip looks correct. If anything seems unclear, you can use the Holiday Pay Calculator below to work out what your rate should be based on your 52‑week average pay.

If you work irregular hours (no fixed days each week), the report also works out your yearly holiday entitlement in hours. It uses the same formula for all leave years: average weekly hours × 5.6 weeks.

How this is labelled depends on when your leave year starts: 12.07% method for leave years starting from April 2024, or the 5.6 weeks method for earlier leave years. For post‑2024 leave years, monthly reports also show how many hours you have built up each pay period. These appear alongside your holiday totals at the end of the year.

What the report checks

When you run a report, the tool checks any payslip that includes holiday hours. These checks apply to all leave years, before and after April 2024.

  • Same‑payslip check: Is the holiday rate lower than your basic hourly rate on the same payslip?
  • 52‑week average check: Is the holiday rate lower than your average hourly rate over the last 52 paid weeks?

If the report flags something, it simply means the numbers are worth a closer look — not that you were definitely underpaid.

If a month contains both ordinary work and holiday, the report may still use it in the 52-week reference when it looks like a mostly-working month. When that happens the report marks the result as low confidence. Treat that as a prompt to ask for your employer’s weekly records, not as a final legal determination.

If you work irregular hours, the report also shows your projected holiday balance in the year‑end totals. The calculation uses the correct method for your leave year and is clearly labelled:

  • Leave years from April 2024: entitlement is based on average weekly hours × 5.6 weeks (12.07% accrual method).
  • Leave years before April 2024: entitlement is based on average weekly hours × 5.6 weeks (5.6‑week method).

Known limitations

  • Monthly payslips, not weekly records. The statutory calculation works week by week. Because payslips are monthly, any month containing statutory sick or maternity pay is excluded in full — the tool cannot split it week by week. This is conservative but means slightly fewer periods may be included in the reference average.
  • Mixed work and holiday months are treated conservatively. If a payslip shows both ordinary working hours and holiday pay, the tool treats that month cautiously when building the 52-week comparison. That does not mean the payslip tells us nothing; it means the monthly format does not show which exact weeks were worked and which were leave, so the report avoids treating that month as a clean reference point.
  • Overtime and regular bonuses are not included. The report uses only your basic hourly pay to build the reference average. If you regularly receive overtime or contractual commission, your true average weekly earnings will be higher — and the report may miss genuine underpayment.
  • No flag does not mean no problem. If you regularly receive overtime or bonuses, verify your holiday pay manually against your full average weekly earnings.

Salaried Workers

What you're entitled to

Salaried workers are entitled to a minimum of 5.6 weeks' paid holiday per year — 28 days for a standard 5-day week, proportionally fewer for part-time. Your holiday pay is simply your normal salary: it continues unchanged during annual leave. The key question is not at what rate you were paid, but how many days were taken and whether your entitlement remains.

How the report estimates days taken

Sage UK payslips record holiday pay as a monetary amount only — no day count is included. The report estimates days taken by working backwards from the amount:

  1. It derives your daily rate from your total basic salary for the year.
  2. It divides the holiday pay amount by that daily rate to estimate days taken.
  3. It subtracts days taken from your entitlement to show days remaining.

NOTE:

Results are approximate (shown with ~). Make sure typical days per week is set correctly in the worker profile — the default is 5, which will produce incorrect results for part-time workers.

Known limitations

  • Constant salary assumed. The daily rate is derived from your total salary for the year. A mid-year pay rise blends two rates together, which can slightly skew the day estimate. If you had a pay rise mid-year, you may want to run two reports, one for each period.
  • No rate underpayment check. For salaried workers your salary is your holiday pay, so the report does not flag whether the rate itself was correct.
  • Part-time settings matter. If you work part-time and the "typical days per week" is left at the default of 5, the day count will be significantly wrong. Set it to your actual contracted days before running the report.

Holiday Pay Calculator (for Hourly Workers)

This calculator helps you work out your holiday pay based on your average earnings over the 52 paid weeks using the 12.07% accrual method.

Use it for pay periods after April 2024, when you want to manually verify your holiday pay, or when the reporter flags something for further review.

What questions does it answer?
  1. "What is a week of holiday worth to me?"
    The correct figure under UK law (average weekly pay from the last 52 paid weeks). This applies to every irregular hours worker regardless of schedule.
  2. "For the X days I took off, how many hours and how much pay should I have received?"
    A convenience estimate for workers who know their typical days per week, letting them cross-check what the payslip shows for a specific holiday period.

Before you start — gather your data
  1. Identify your last 52 weeks of normal pay — not necessarily the last 52 calendar weeks. Skip any week where you received only Statutory Sick Pay (SSP), Statutory Maternity Pay (SMP), Statutory Paternity Pay (SPP), adoption pay, or no pay at all, replacing it with the next earlier paid week (going back up to 104 weeks if needed).
  2. Flat rate? Add up your total hours across those 52 weeks and use Hours & rate mode. Include overtime hours only if they are paid at the same rate as ordinary hours.
  3. Variable pay? If you had premium overtime, bonuses, commission, or a pay rise during this period, add up your total gross earnings (before tax and deductions) for those same 52 eligible weeks and use Total Gross Pay mode instead.

The legally correct entitlement is a week's pay per week of holiday. The per-day figures are a convenience estimate only.

Resources

If you think you've been underpaid

  • Ask your employer for their week-by-week pay records. Under the Employment Rights Act 1996, they are legally required to provide these on request.
  • Seek help from Citizens Advice if you're unsure how to proceed.
  • Contact ACAS for free, impartial advice on pay disputes.
